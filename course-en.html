Datunwen — A First Course

A constructed language where the melody of your voice does the grammar. Eight short lessons. No linguistics degree required — if you can hear the difference between “You saw the cat.” and “You saw the cat?”, you already have every skill this language asks for.

LESSON 0

Welcome

Orientation

Datunwen (“Omnilingua”) is an engineered world language: sixteen consonants that nearly every human being can pronounce, a few hundred self-explaining roots, and one signature invention — grammar carried on pitch. This page is a complete beginner’s course; here is the whole design philosophy in three cards.

AXIOM Ⅰ

Two channels, one mouth

Your tongue and lips spell out the words; at the same time, the pitch of your voice — controlled by the larynx, almost independently — tags each word’s job in the sentence. Grammar rides a parallel channel, so not one syllable is wasted on it.

AXIOM Ⅱ

Only pay for surprises

In the default word order, nothing is marked — zero endings, zero little words. Only when you rearrange a sentence for emphasis does a one-syllable tag appear, exactly where it is needed. Grammar priced like information.

AXIOM Ⅲ

One word, one roof

A word is one pitch melody in the ear, one roofed block-cluster on the page, one unit in the grammar. What you hear, what you see, and what you parse are three views of the same object.

Watch the two spelling traps: x is always “sh” (never “ks”), and c is always “ch” (never “k” or “s”).

THE FRIENDLY PAIRS · p/b · t/d · k/g

How do p and b differ? Your choice. Distinguish them the English way (puff of air: pie vs buy) or the Spanish/French way (voiceless vs voiced) — Datunwen accepts either. The pairs are designed so that almost any accent in the world lands on the right side.

ONE LIQUID, TWO LETTERS · r / l

The letters r and l spell one single sound. Say it as an English r, an l, or a quick Spanish tap — all correct, and no two words are ever distinguished by r versus l. Each word’s spelling is fixed (it is always ren, never “len”), but your pronunciation is free. Half the world’s learners just exhaled.

1.3 One possible ending: -n

A syllable may end in -n — and in nothing else. Say it as “n”, as “ng”, or just nasalize the vowel (like casual English “-in’ ” for “-ing”): all three count as the same thing. Try:

THE WHOLE SOUND SYSTEM IN ONE LINE

A syllable = one consonant + one vowel (+ optional -n). That’s it. No clusters — no “str-”, no “-mp”, ever. 16 consonants × 9 vowel choices × 2 (with or without -n) = 288 possible syllables.

One footnote: every content word begins with a consonant. Only the little grammar particles may be bare vowels (e, i, o, u, a…) — which conveniently makes them instantly recognizable as grammar.

LESSON 2

The pitch trick — melody is the grammar

The Signature Invention

Say these two English sentences aloud: “You saw the cat.” — voice falls. “You saw the cat?” — voice rises. Same words; the melody alone changed the meaning. English spends that melodic channel on attitude. Datunwen spends it on grammar.

Here is the whole system. Every content word is spoken with one pitch contour stretched over the entire word, and that contour announces the word’s job in the sentence — noun-job, verb-job, or modifier-job. The dictionary stores no parts of speech at all; you assign them, live, with your voice:

Level high · thing (spelling: unmarked)
The word is being used as a thing — subject or object, the noun slot. Like flatly listing items.
Falling · verb (spelling: add -q)
The word is the statement itself — the verb of the clause. It is the “full stop” melody of an English declarative: “Done.”
Rising · modifier (spelling: add -z)
The word modifies what comes next — adjective or adverb slot. Rising = “hang on, more coming”, exactly English’s mid-sentence continuation rise.
Low flat · particle (closed set of 23)
The 23 little grammar words — case tags, connectives, time anchors — all low, short and quiet, like English’s unstressed “of”, “the”, “and”.
THE MUTE LETTERS q AND z

In spelling, word-final q means “say this word with a falling contour”, and z means “rising”. They are never pronounced as consonants — kaq is “ka” with a falling voice, not “kak”; kaz is “ka” rising, not “kazz”. Think of them as a question-mark or full stop that happens to sit inside the word.

NOTHING TO MEMORIZE — REALLY

This is not like Mandarin, where every word carries an arbitrary tone you must memorize forever. Here pitch carries zero dictionary information — it is three grammar rules, total, applied to any word. It is far closer to English question intonation than to Chinese tones: you already do this; Datunwen just made it official.

LESSON 3

Your first sentences

Syntax I

You know the sounds and the melodies. That is genuinely all you need — here is a real sentence.

Read it like a native: mau “cat” rides level (it’s a thing — the subject). canq falls (it’s the verb: “eats”). le is a low little particle meaning “completed” (Lesson 4 explains it — for now it makes the eating finished: “ate”). pei “fish” rides level again (a thing — the object).

RULE R2 · DEFAULT ORDER

Subject – Verb – Object, exactly like English. And every modifier comes before what it modifies, like English “big cat”. Stay in this order and — by rule R3 — you mark nothing at all: no endings, no case tags, zero.

3.1 Asking — the particle u

To ask a yes/no question, put the low particle u at the end — a spoken question mark. (Meet the pronouns while you’re here: mi “I”, yu “you”, ta “he/she/it” — and the pointing word to “that”. Pronouns are ordinary content words: they carry contours like everything else.)

3.2 Denying — the particle bu

Negation is bu “not” before the verb. (Etymology note: from Chinese 不 bù.)

3.3 Bending the order — and paying one syllable

Want to lead with the fish, English-style: “That fish — the cat ate it.”? Fine. But the fish has now left its default slot, so — rule R3 — it takes a tag: wo, “this one is the patient (the done-to)”.

There are exactly three such tags, used only when word order deviates: ga = agent (the doer), wo = patient (the done-to), ni = to/for (recipient or goal). (Etymology note: borrowed from Japanese が·を·に.) In default SVO order they never appear — that is Axiom Ⅱ in action.

LESSON 4

Time travel — set the scene once

Tense & Aspect

English stamps tense onto every single verb: saw, ate, will see, will eat… Datunwen refuses to pay per verb. You set the scene once — like a novel’s “Once upon a time” — and every sentence after it lives there until you say otherwise.

The three time anchors are low particles: tan = past, nau = present, fu = future. (Etymology notes: cf. French antan “yesteryear”, English now / Latin nunc, Latin futūrus.) Drop one at the start of a stretch of talk and it scopes: it binds every following clause until you re-anchor. Optionally, two aspect particles ride right after the verb: le = completed (etymology note: Chinese 了 le) and xu = still going (Chinese 续 xù “continue”).

Two new pieces: an is the “at (a time)” particle, and tandi “yesterday” is a fossilized compound — tan past + di day = “past-day”. So the opening literally says “at past-day” — and that phrase is the anchor for everything that follows.

4.1 The explicitness dial — three legal registers

Formal speech spells everything out; between friends, context already carries most of it. All three rows below are fully grammatical and mean the same thing — explicitness in Datunwen is a dial, not a fate:

4.2 Future, right-now, and the free ride

And the payoff — anchors are inherited. One anchor, two clauses, and the second verb pays nothing (i = “and”, a low particle):

TIMELESS IS LEGAL

Definitions, laws of nature, proverbs — statements true at all times — take no anchor at all: mau canq pei “cats eat fish”. That silence is not laziness; it is the accurate choice, and rule R4 licenses it. Keep this in mind for the Declaration of Human Rights in Lesson 7.

EXERCISE 4 · Time
  1. Say “I will see you.”
    Show answer
    fu mi kanq yu. — future anchor first, then plain SVO.
  2. What is the difference in meaning between mau canq pei and nau mau canq xu pei?
    Show answer
    mau canq pei is timeless/generic: “cats eat fish”. nau mau canq xu pei is anchored to the present with durative xu: “the cat is eating the fish right now”.
  3. True or false: after opening a story with tan, you must repeat tan before every verb.
    Show answer
    False — that is the whole point. An anchor scopes over everything that follows until you set a new one. English re-marks tense on every verb; Datunwen pays once per scene.
  4. Give two ways — one formal, one relaxed — to say “Yesterday you saw the fish.”
    Show answer
    Formal: an tandi, yu kanq le pei. Relaxed: tandi yu kanq pei. (Sentence-initial time word = anchor; le implied.)
  5. What exactly does le add to a verb, and where does it sit?
    Show answer
    It marks the action as completed (perfective) and sits immediately after the verb: canq le “ate / has eaten”. Etymology note: borrowed from Chinese 了.

5.2 The folding rule: head on the right

Compounds are [modifier + head] — the right-hand root names the kind of thing, the left-hand root narrows it down. English already works this way: a bookshop is a shop, a catfish is a fish. Fold once:

su “self” ＋ ka “know” ↓ fold

Folding is recursive — a compound can be folded into a bigger compound, like tunseitai above: [tun+sei “co-exist”] + tai “state” = “the state of existing together”. A physics term you can decode with no physics.

THE su- FAMILY — A PHILOSOPHY IN THREE WORDS

sukau freedom = self + cause: that which is its own cause (Spinoza’s causa sui — and character-for-character the Chinese 自由, “from-self”). sudin dignity = self + worth: worth you have by being, not by grant. sulindin rights = [self + link] + worth: the worth recognized in the web between persons — rights exist only in community. Lesson 7 puts all three in one famous sentence.

5.4 Gluing phrases: e and ye

Two low particles attach modifiers to a following noun — and because there are two of them, a classic ambiguity dies:

  • e attaches a noun phrase — English ’s. (Etymology note: Chinese 的 de, Japanese の no.)
  • ye attaches a whole clause — English that… / who…. (Etymology note: classical Chinese 者 zhě.)

Now the famous pair. English “I saw the man with the telescope” never resolves who has the telescope. Datunwen cannot even write the ambiguity:

One letter of difference, two unmistakably different sentences. And you can nest as deep as you like: mi kanq le canq le pei ye mau — “I saw the cat that ate the fish.”

EXERCISE 5 · Word-building
  1. Build “history” out of ten “time” and lin “link”.
    Show answer
    tenlin — modifier first, head last: “time-links”. (linten would be some sort of time belonging to links!)
  2. Which single root is the head of sulindin “rights” — and so, what kind of thing does the word say rights fundamentally are?
    Show answer
    The rightmost root din “worth”. Rights are a kind of worth — specifically, [self-link]-worth: worth arising from the links between selves.
  3. Translate both: mau e pei and pei e mau.
    Show answer
    mau e pei = “the cat’s fish”; pei e mau = “the fish’s cat”. X e Y always reads “X’s Y” — modifier before head, like everything in this language.

    Show answer

  5. Without looking back: su = self, ka = know. What is suka, and which classical word has the identical anatomy?
    Show answer
    suka = consciousness, “self-knowing” — the same two pieces as Latin con-scientia.

The thin grey square around each block is not part of the writing. It is a learner’s grid — like the ruled squares in a child’s handwriting workbook — kept on in this course so you can see each syllable’s boundaries. Fluent text drops it. Nothing structural rests on it.

EXERCISE 6 · Reading
  1. Scrolling back to any sentence line: how do you spot the verb without reading a single letter?
    Show answer
    Find the block-cluster whose roof falls from left down to right. One falling roof = the clause’s predicate.
  2. What does the small red mark in the bottom-left of a block tell you, and how is it pronounced?
    Show answer

Plain i “and” would claim the cat is dead and alive — a contradiction. THE SILENCE THAT IS PRECISE

No time anchor anywhere — and that is correct. This is a generic, normative statement, true at all moments; rule R4 legally leaves it unmarked. English is forced to write “are born”; Datunwen’s tenselessness says exactly what the Declaration means. Reading the three words is reading the argument.

EXERCISE 7 · Final
  1. In the Schrödinger sentence, name the verb and the two independent ways you can find it.
    Show answer
    cuq “is-located”. By ear: the only falling contour in the sentence. By eye: the only falling roof (and in spelling, the only -q).
  2. Why does the sentence use io instead of i or o?
    Show answer
    i would assert “dead and alive” (contradiction); o would assert “dead or alive, one of them” (what quantum mechanics denies). io ⊕ asserts the options as one jointly-held whole — superposition, straight from the grammar.
  3. In the UDHR sentence, which two words are the predicates, and how does your ear know?
    Show answer
    sukauq “is-free” and pinq “is-equal” — the two falling contours (the two -q’s).
  4. Why does en stand before Xedinga, and what visual mark does the name get in the script?
    Show answer
    en is the proper-name marker: it announces that the next blocks are a foreign name spelled by sound. In the script the name carries a dotted red name-line beneath its blocks.
  5. Why is the UDHR sentence allowed to have no tense at all — and what would adding tan wrongly imply?
    Show answer
    It is a timeless normative statement, and rule R4 licenses zero anchoring for statements true at all times. Adding tan would anchor it to the past — as if people used to be born free and equal.
